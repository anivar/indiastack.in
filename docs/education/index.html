DIKSHA Data Breach

Government Claims

Secure educational platform. Essential learning tool during COVID. Data protected. Serving millions of students.

Documented Reality

Exposed 600K students' data for over a year. Advertising trackers. Location data collection. No response to HRW warnings.

Data Exposed (March 2020 - December 2022)

  • • Children's full names
  • • School names
  • • State, district, block location
  • • Test scores
  • • Partially redacted phone numbers
  • • Partially redacted email addresses
  • HRW Investigation (2023): For over a year, DIKSHA exposed personal data of ~600,000 students and 1 million teachers on the open web.
  • Unprotected Cloud Server: Microsoft Azure server left unprotected. Anyone could access the data.
  • Location Tracking: HRW found DIKSHA had capacity to collect children's precise location data including current and last known location.
  • Advertising Trackers: DIKSHA transmitted children's data to Google through trackers designed for advertising uses.
  • No Response: HRW wrote to education ministry in March, May, and July 2022. Received no response.
  • NCERT Denial: In RTI response, NCERT denied location tracking allegations, contradicting HRW's technical findings.
  • Mandatory During COVID: Students compelled to use DIKSHA as their only means of education during school closures.

APAAR No Legal Backing

Government Claims

Voluntary enrollment. One Nation One Student ID. Seamless transitions between institutions. Academic achievement tracking.

Documented Reality

No statutory backing. Mandatory in practice. Lifelong surveillance registry. Mass enrollment without informed consent.

  • No Legal Basis: APAAR has no legal or statutory backing, based only on a letter from the Secretary of Education.
  • "Voluntary" in Name Only: Union government says optional, but states threaten schools for non-compliance. UP warned of derecognizing madrassas.
  • DPDPA Violation: Section 9(3) prohibits "tracking or behavioural monitoring of children", APAAR appears to violate this.
  • Lifelong Registry: Creates "permanent, life-long, longitudinal registry" of all education and extracurricular data.
  • RTI Stonewalling: When IFF filed RTI, government transferred application over 30 times with no clear answers for months.
  • Mass Enrollment: Schools directed to enroll students en masse after "forcing parents' consent.", Mishi Choudhary, SFLC.in
  • Third-Party Risk: Open APIs and data sharing channels without real safeguards expose children's data to third parties.
  • Profiling Risk: Researchers warn of profiling and surveillance risks from centralized educational repository.

State-Level Coercion

Despite "voluntary" claims:

  • University of Mumbai made APAAR mandatory for college allocation, SFLC.in wrote objecting
  • Uttar Pradesh threatens to derecognize schools not generating APAAR IDs
  • NEET exams pushing APAAR enrollment despite NTA claiming it's voluntary
  • Goa's digital integration push made APAAR practically mandatory

2025

States pressure schools into mass enrollment. "Voluntary on paper but mandatory in practice."

The Pattern: Children as Data Subjects

"In the absence of legal sanction, it is worrisome to see that schools are being directed to enroll students en masse after forcing parents' consent."
- Mishi Choudhary, Founder, SFLC.in

Children have no bargaining power. They cannot refuse. Their data is captured before they understand what data means.

  • No Special Protections: Law says protect children's data. Systems designed to collect it anyway.
  • Coercive Consent: Sign the form or your child doesn't get admitted. That's not consent. That's leverage.
  • Lifelong Profiles: Data from age 6 follows you forever. A registry that never forgets, never forgives, never deletes.
  • No Exit: Once enrolled, you cannot leave. No delete button. No opt-out. The system remembers.
  • Closed Systems: What data? How used? Who sees it? Code is closed. You cannot verify anything.

What Parents Can Do

SFLC.in provides a template letter for parents who wish to opt out of APAAR enrollment. However, even this option is being undermined as states make enrollment practically mandatory.

The fundamental issue: parents should not need to actively resist a system that lacks legal backing and violates data protection principles. The burden is wrongly placed on citizens to protect themselves from their own government's data collection.
