Why Corrigibility Is a Structural Necessity

A condition for stability in population-scale systems.

The Structural Problem

DPI runs at population scale. Rules are encoded in advance. Access to essential services goes through these systems. They will inevitably misclassify, exclude, or fail some users some of the time. Not a bug. Math.

Errors will happen. The question is: can those affected detect, correct, and reverse them before harm becomes permanent?

Corrigibility is the system property that determines this.

NOTATION NOTE

Simple mathematical expressions are used here to state structural relationships compactly. No formal training is required; each expression is explained in plain language.

Control, Variety, and Feedback

Claim: No fixed rule system can regulate population-scale diversity without feedback from those affected.

Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety states that a regulator must possess at least as much variety as the disturbances it seeks to regulate.

Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety
V(controller) ≥ V(disturbance)

In DPI, the disturbance is human diversity across language, mobility, disability, geography, documentation, and life events. The controller is a pre-specified rule system.

No fixed rule set can match population variety. Stability therefore depends on feedback channels that transmit error signals from affected populations back into system behavior.

Learned Systems (AI)

In learned systems, the controller is implemented by a trained mapping fθ. Training fixes θ until retraining occurs, while the real-world distribution P(X; t) continues to change.

As a result, the controller's effective variety is fixed, while disturbance variety can increase over time. The variety gap does not close. It widens.

The five corrigibility tests define whether feedback channels exist to compensate for this structural limitation.

OPERATIONAL TRANSLATION

Each test corresponds to a distinct, demonstrable feedback or governance channel. EXIT provides refusal signals; CODE provides legibility; AUDIT provides independent error measurement; GOVERN provides collective rule-change; FORK provides credible replacement. The Exposure Surfaces (annex) lists the minimal artifacts that prove these channels exist.

How Systems Lose Requisite Variety

Claim: Each failed corrigibility test removes a feedback channel and reduces control capacity.

EXIT blocked Refusal impossible. No negative signal from non-participation.
CODE hidden Decision logic unobservable. Errors cannot be inspected.
AUDIT restricted External verification blocked. Error rates remain unknown.
GOVERN excluded Rules cannot be adjusted by those affected.
FORK prohibited No credible replacement. Error persistence becomes rational.

When these channels are blocked, V(controller) falls below V(disturbance). The system cannot regulate its own impact.

Instability is guaranteed.

Constitutional Preconditions

Claim: Corrigibility supplies what constitutional review presupposes but cannot enforce.

Proportionality analysis, the foundation of rights review across jurisdictions, presupposes conditions in order to function:

  • Legibility of rules, power must be knowable to be contestable
  • Reversibility of outcomes, harm must be correctable
  • Availability of alternatives, necessity collapses without choice
  • Independent review, oversight cannot depend on operator permission

Where digital systems block these structurally, courts cannot apply review meaningfully. Review becomes contingent on operator assertion. Oversight shifts from verification to trust.

Corrigibility supplies the structural preconditions that law presupposes but cannot itself enforce.

The False Commons: Why DPI Is Not a Public Good

Claim: A system that fails commons governance conditions is an enclosure, not a public good.

Elinor Ostrom identified the conditions under which commons can be sustainably governed. Digital Public Infrastructure claims to be a commons for the digital age.

It fails these conditions.

BOUNDARIES Must be knowable. In DPI, the boundary between public service and private data extraction is opaque.
MONITORING Must be accountable to users. DPI inverts this. Citizens are fully visible to the system. The system is opaque to citizens.
SANCTIONS Must be graduated. DPI imposes binary outcomes. Authentication failure leads to exclusion.
DISPUTES Must be resolved locally and at low cost. DPI centralizes redress or requires litigation, making errors permanent for many.
RULE-MAKING Must involve those affected. DPI rules are set through executive or technocratic processes.

Training Data as a Commons (AI)

In AI systems, training data is an upstream commons. It is collectively produced, socially generated, and unevenly governed. When enclosed without reciprocal governance, systems inherit the legitimacy of the commons without its constraints.

A system that fails these conditions is not a commons. It is an enclosure.

Limits of Openness

Claim: Formal openness does not guarantee functional correction.

Free and Open Source Software enables correction by making power observable and replaceable. The four freedoms (use, study, modify, distribute) exist because software that cannot be inspected cannot be trusted.

But openness has layers.

For traditional DPI, source code access often suffices for legibility.

For AI systems, code may be visible while training data, model weights, or optimization regimes remain inaccessible. Even where artifacts are disclosed, scale and emergence may limit interpretability.

Corrigibility depends on whether openness enables actual replacement and correction, not on formal disclosure alone.

Open weights without training data replicate the problem of open standards without open execution.

Temporal Persistence

Claim: Corrigibility must hold across time, not merely at deployment.

Traditional DPI systems are static. A database persists. Rules are fixed. What is deployed is what runs.

AI systems evolve. Retraining changes behavior. Updates are silent. Drift occurs without notice.

A system corrigible at deployment may become incorrigible over time.

From a cybernetic perspective, this introduces temporal instability. Safeguards validated at launch may erode as systems change. Feedback channels designed for one version may not reach the next.

Temporal Variety Gap
Δ(t) = V(disturbance; t) − V(controller; θ)
The variety gap grows when θ is fixed and the world continues to change.

Corrigibility is not a deployment property. It is a persistence condition.

For learned systems, corrigibility depends on detection-to-remediation latency; auditors must see both detection triggers and remediation windows.

Grievance Is Not a Feedback Channel

Claim: Expression without causal influence does not correct systems.

Many incorrigible systems provide grievance mechanisms. These absorb complaints without altering system behavior.

Grievance Absorption
∂S / ∂G = 0
System state is invariant to grievance volume.

Grievance channels record dissatisfaction. They do not modify execution, policy, or eligibility logic. They therefore do not increase controller variety.

Corrigibility requires causal influence, not expression. The tests measure whether affected populations can change system state, not whether they can complain.

The Exit Threshold

There exists a structural threshold beyond which authority becomes coercive rather than legitimate.

When refusal does not trigger exclusion

Consent remains meaningful.

When refusal triggers material harm

Consent collapses.

Once this threshold is crossed, other safeguards become discretionary rather than enforceable.

Conservation of Correction Demand

Claim: Blocking designed correction channels does not reduce correction demand, it displaces it.

Correction demand is conserved.

Conservation Law
Fdesigned + Femergent = K
Fdesigned = correction flow through designed channels Femergent = correction flow through emergent channels K = total correction demand (constant)

The system chooses where correction occurs; it cannot choose whether it occurs.

When designed correction channels are blocked, correction does not disappear. It reroutes through emergent channels: protest, courts, journalism, political pressure, informal workarounds.

Emergent correction is slower, costlier, and more destabilizing.

Blocking designed channels shifts cost from operators to affected populations, and eventually back to operators through legitimacy collapse.

Interpreting the Schemas

Two complementary JSON schemas separate operator facts from auditor judgments. This is one approach to make corrigibility surfaces legible; other mechanisms (statutory registers, public dashboards) are equally valid.

INFRASTRUCTURE.JSON

Operator Manifest

Published by system operators. Declares operational facts: dependencies, access channels, governance model. Does not assert compliance.

Analogy: package.json

CORRIGIBILITY.JSON

Auditor Assessment

Authored by auditors, not operators. Evaluates structural correction capacity against the five tests. Contains pass/fail judgments with evidence.

Analogy: security audit report

Operators declare facts; auditors render verdicts. Operators cannot grade their own homework.

STRUCTURAL READING

Boolean fields force confession.

A system declaring offline_equivalent: false admits EXIT failure. Simple true/false fields cannot hide structural capture.

Named dependencies enable scrutiny.

closed_components: ["oracle-db", "idemia-abis"] is more useful than proprietary: 3. Names allow investigation.

SLOs as justice metrics.

dispute_slo: "30d" and fix_slo: "90d" quantify the velocity of justice. Operators must commit to response times.

Auditor assessment fields:

  • penalty_ratio: Time/cost ratio of manual vs digital. 1 = equal, 999 = blocked.
  • visibility: CODE enum: full, partial, api_only, none.
  • user_power: GOVERN enum: binding, veto, advisory, none.
  • barriers: FORK array: legal, technical, economic, regulatory, data.

The schemas make implicit claims explicit. Comparing manifests across systems reveals differences that promotional language obscures.

SCHEMA STRUCTURE

INFRASTRUCTURE.JSON v1.4

  • meta: system_id, deployment, lifecycle
  • operations: status_page, slos{uptime_target, dispute_resolution}
  • governance: model, escalation_paths[], public_participation
  • components: repositories[], closed_components[]
  • learned_components: [{name, role, when_uncertain}]
  • access: endpoints[], offline_channels[], offline_equivalent

CORRIGIBILITY.JSON v1.4

  • target: matches infrastructure system_id
  • assessed_by: auditor organization
  • assessed_at: required date (closes temporal loophole)
  • tests: exit, code, audit, govern, fork (with evidence[])
  • verdict: corrigible, tests_passed, summary

WHAT THIS MANIFEST CANNOT DO

Link Rot Escape Hatch

A URL satisfies the schema even if it points to a 404, a login screen, or a blank PDF. Schema validates syntax, not semantics. Independent verification is required.

Self-Attestation Limits

The manifest is self-declared. Without independent crawler/verifier infrastructure, claims remain unverified. The manifest enables scrutiny; it does not perform it.

Subjective Fields

service_equivalence: "full" is defined by the operator. An 8-hour queue may be called "full equivalence" by those who never stand in it.

The manifest is a Warrant Canary, not a Compliance Certificate.

  • If a system refuses to publish: they are hiding something.
  • If they publish honestly: they admit structural flaws.
  • If they publish dishonestly: evidence_surfaces allow civil society to catch the lie.

ON CAPACITY TO EXERCISE

These tests do not require every individual to read code or audit models. They require that someone independent can.

A farmer cannot audit a neural network. But the farmer needs a world where journalists, researchers, and civil society organizations can, and where their findings have consequence.

Corrigibility is not individual capacity. It is structural possibility.

Conclusion

Corrigibility is a structural condition for stability in systems that exercise asymmetric power at population scale.

This applies to deterministic infrastructures and to learned systems. In the latter case, correction is harder because behavior is produced through training while environmental variation continues.

Corrigibility is a structural necessity: systems that cannot be corrected by those they affect will be corrected by forces they cannot control.

Practically, corrigibility is not a slogan. It is a set of demonstrable channels. The Exposure Surfaces (annex) shows the minimal artifacts needed to prove those channels are present.

Corrigibility must therefore be defined, tested, built, and required across all infrastructures that claim public status, including Digital Public Infrastructure and large-scale AI systems.

Not as ethics. Not as preference. As structure.
