DPI Corrigibility Framework

Universal criteria for evaluating Digital Public Infrastructure.
Five tests. All must pass.

Public infrastructure must be corrigible by those it affects.

Corrigibility combines three necessary capacities: observable feedback (cybernetics), participatory rule-making (commons governance), and credible replaceability (free software freedoms). The five tests measure whether these capacities are present. Failure of any single test disqualifies a system from DPI.

Where this condition is not met, the system may be widely used or state-backed, but it does not operate as public infrastructure.

Corrigibility is not a governance preference or ethical aspiration.
It is a structural condition for legitimacy in systems that exercise power at population scale.

The Problem: International Definitions Lack Structure

Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) is being deployed globally at unprecedented speed. Governments and multilateral institutions increasingly rely on digital identity systems, payment rails, data exchanges, and now large-scale AI to mediate access to essential services.

The G20 (2023) and the United Nations have adopted official definitions that now shape procurement, funding, and policy alignment across jurisdictions. These definitions articulate desirable attributes, open, inclusive, interoperable, accountable, but do not specify structural conditions under which those attributes can be verified or enforced.

G20 NEW DELHI DECLARATION (2023)
"Digital public infrastructure… is described as a set of shared digital systems that should be secure and interoperable, and can be built on open standards and specifications to deliver and provide equitable access to public and/or private services at societal scale and are governed by applicable legal frameworks and enabling rules."
UN / UNDP (2023–2024)
"Digital public infrastructure refers to solutions and systems that enable the effective provision of essential society-wide functions… DPI can be provided by the public or private sector."

The same institutions now promote "AI as Digital Public Infrastructure" using identical language, without introducing additional structural requirements.

Why These Definitions Are Insufficient

✗ Aspirational language is not definitional, "should be" and "can be" describe intent, not conditions

✗ Open standards are not open execution, published specifications do not reveal how systems operate

✗ "Applicable legal frameworks" exclude nothing, all systems operate under some legal regime

✗ No verification mechanism exists, compliance is self-asserted

Without structural requirements, "public" becomes a descriptive label rather than an operational property.

The Five Structural Tests

All five are necessary. All must pass.

Failure of any one test disqualifies a system from evaluation as genuinely public infrastructure.

TEST 01: SAFE REFUSAL (EXIT)

Can a person decline participation while retaining access to essential services?

Pass: Refusal carries no penalty. Non-digital or alternative pathways remain available.

Fail: Opting out results in exclusion from food, banking, healthcare, employment, or mobility.

Where refusal triggers material harm, consent cannot be considered freely exercised.

TEST 02: LEGIBLE EXECUTION (CODE)

Is the system's actual execution observable in practice?

Pass: Executing logic is inspectable. Source code or equivalent execution artifacts are available.

Fail: Only policy documents, APIs, or specifications are public.

For systems with learned components, legibility extends to training processes, data composition, and optimization regimes, not only inference interfaces.

TEST 03: INDEPENDENT VERIFICATION (AUDIT)

Can independent parties verify system behavior without operator permission?

Pass: External auditors can measure error rates, bias, and failure modes. Results are public.

Fail: Verification depends on operator consent or is legally restricted.

Benchmark compliance does not substitute for deployment-level verification.

TEST 04: CONSTITUTIVE INPUT (GOVERN)

Do affected populations have binding authority in system design and evolution?

Pass: Governance includes structured, pre-deployment participation by those affected.

Fail: Decisions are made internally. Consultation, if any, is advisory or post hoc.

Grievance mechanisms after harm do not replace authority before deployment.

TEST 05: CREDIBLE REPLACEMENT (FORK)

Can the system be replicated or replaced without incumbent permission?

Pass: Alternative implementations are legally, technically, and economically feasible.

Fail: Control is centralized. Barriers prevent credible alternatives.

For capital-intensive systems, economic concentration (compute, data, infrastructure) constitutes a structural barrier even absent formal licensing restrictions.

Failure of any one test is fatal.

Partial compliance does not count. These are necessary conditions, not a scoring rubric.

Open-Washing Patterns

How systems claim openness while maintaining structural closure.

These patterns recur across Digital Public Infrastructure and AI systems. They are not implementation accidents. They are predictable strategies for satisfying the language of openness while defeating its substance.

01

API-as-Openness

CODE

Interface ≠ system

Publishing endpoint documentation while keeping execution proprietary.

DPI: APIs published; core resolution opaque

AI: API docs; training data undisclosed

02

Spec-as-Source

FORK CODE

Readable ≠ replicable

DPI: Open specs; one operator

Funnel Open-Sourcing

FORK GOVERN

Open-sourcing SDKs while keeping control components closed.

DPI: Open frontend; closed biometrics

AI: Open fine-tuning; closed base models

04

DID-Washing

EXIT FORK

Local storage ≠ decentralized trust

Using decentralized vocabulary with centralized trust roots.

Publishing safety principles, benchmarks, or red-team summaries without independent audit access.

AI: Benchmark compliance cited without disclosure of scope, caveats, or deployment variance.

DPI: Internal risk assessments presented as accountability.

Open-washing enables partial compliance narratives. The corrigibility framework rejects partial compliance by design.

Analytical Foundations

This framework converges three independent traditions that arrive at the same requirements:

CYBERNETICS

Legitimate control requires feedback from those affected. Systems that suppress error signals drift toward failure.

COMMONS (OSTROM)

Shared systems require user participation in rule-making, monitoring, and conflict resolution. Absent these, commons become enclosures.

FREE SOFTWARE

Inspectability and forkability discipline power. Openness without these properties is rhetorical.

AI systems stress all three by compressing social variety, enclosing collectively produced data, and centralizing execution through capital-intensive infrastructure.

The DPI Corrigibility Framework does not assume that all technologies are ready to govern populations.

It exists to identify when they are not.

That is why corrigibility must be defined, tested, built, and required, across identity systems, payment rails, data infrastructures, and learning systems that claim public status.

Not as ethics. Not as preference. As structure.
