The Architecture

India's Payments Stack is cited as DPI's success story. Look closer: 51% public ownership, Rs 8,730 crore in government subsidies, CAG-appointed auditors, yet claims exemption from RTI. A private consortium takes public money but rejects public accountability. One node approves all participants. You cannot exit.

The Accountability Contradiction

CAG appoints NPCI's auditors (government company). Public sector banks hold 51%+ shares. Government gave Rs 8,730 crore in subsidies. Yet NPCI claims it is NOT a public authority and NOT subject to RTI. It takes public money but rejects public accountability.

  • Designed as Monopoly: 2011 Technical Advisory Group (Nandan Nilekani) acknowledged: "upfront sunk-cost, economies of scale, and network externalities" make it a natural monopoly.
  • No Competition Allowed: RBI rejected all New Umbrella Entity (NUE) applications from Reliance, Axis, Tata, Paytm, on "flimsy grounds" (MediaNama).
  • Opaque Decision-Making: Cook and Raman research: scheme rules set by product-level committees, no public consultation, no recommendations sought.
  • Quasi-Regulator Role: NPCI trying to play competition regulator with 30% market cap, while also being a participant.

RBI Regulatory Failures

The Reserve Bank of India has failed on three counts with payments infrastructure:

  • Failed Independent Oversight: RBI signed off on Watal Committee recommendation for a separate Payments Regulator, then switched to "self regulation", retaining control without establishing independent oversight.
  • Failed to Enable Competition: RBI teased New Umbrella Entities (NUEs), received applications from Reliance, Axis, Tata, Paytm, then rejected all on grounds as thin as "we did not see innovation." NPCI monopoly continues.
  • Failed Public Accountability: No public consultation for critical payments infrastructure. No recommendations sought from civil society or merchant community.

Until there is competition, NPCI remains a single point of failure for digital payments in India. And a systemic risk.

  • Marketplace Owner Competing: NPCI runs UPI AND owns BHIM app. Participants who pay NPCI fees are effectively funding their own competition.
  • Spun Off, Still Controlled: BHIM was spun off as fully owned subsidiary, but NPCI gave it Rs 300 crore to attract customers (Moneycontrol, Oct 2024).
  • "National Initiative" Misrepresentation: NPCI CEO called BHIM a "national initiative", despite NPCI winning CIC case to confirm it is NOT a government body.
  • Third-Party Data Sharing: BHIM privacy policy: data shared with "financial institutions, service providers, merchants or regulatory/government authorities."
  • Aadhaar Linkage: BHIM enables Aadhaar-based authentication, linking financial transactions to biometric identity.

Despite Rs 300 crore in funding, BHIM has just 0.2% market share. PhonePe (Walmart) and Google Pay control 85.9%. One duopoly (Visa/Mastercard) was replaced with another, on "open" public infrastructure.

Recent Security Measures (2025)

NPCI has introduced new security guidelines, but fundamental issues remain:

  • API Security Guidelines (May 2025): New OC-215 mandate for PSPs. Implementation deadline July 2025.
  • Consent for Seeding/Porting: Now requires explicit consent (March 2025), after years of violations.
  • Biometric Authentication: Cut security incidents by 58%, but increases surveillance capability.
  • Federated Learning: NPCI piloting fraud detection without sharing customer data, step in right direction but limited.

These improvements address symptoms while leaving the fundamental architecture, private control of public infrastructure, unchanged.

The Pattern: Financial Enclosure

You cannot exit. Cash is disappearing. Every transaction is visible. The enclosure is complete.

  • No Exit: Cash is disappearing. Try paying rent without UPI. Try receiving salary without a bank. The perimeter closes.
  • Total Visibility: Every rupee you spend. Every rupee you receive. Visible to your bank, NPCI, government, and whoever they share with.
  • Gatekeeping Node: NPCI, a private company, decides who can offer payment services. One node controls all flow.
  • Consent Theater: Sign the terms or don't use the service. That's not consent. That's compulsion dressed as choice.
  • Export Model: This is what India sells abroad as "Digital Public Infrastructure." Private control of public rails. Surveillance by design.
